### Chapter VIII
# The Queen’s Croquet-Ground

A large rose-tree stood near the entrance of the garden, with roses that were initially white, but there were three gardeners busily painting them red. Alice found this very curious and stepped closer to watch them. Just as she approached, she heard one of them say, ‘Look out now, Five! Don’t splash paint all over me like that!’

‘I couldn’t help it,’ replied Five, sulkily. ‘Seven jogged my elbow.’

To which Seven retorted, ‘That’s right, Five! Always blame others!’

‘*You* had better not talk!’ countered Five. ‘I heard the Queen say just yesterday that you deserved to be beheaded!’

‘What for?’ asked the first gardener.

‘That’s none of *your* business, Two!’ said Seven.

‘Actually, it *is* his business!’ declared Five. ‘And I’ll tell him — it was for bringing the cook tulip-roots instead of onions.’

Seven threw down his brush and began, ‘Well, of all the unfair things —’ when he noticed Alice watching them. He stopped abruptly, and the others turned and bowed low.

![Two, Five, and Seven painting the rosebush](000011.png)

‘Would you tell me,’ Alice asked timidly, ‘why you are painting those roses?’

Five and Seven remained silent, looking at Two, who began softly, ‘Well, you see, Miss, this was supposed to be a *red* rose-tree, but we mistakenly put in a white one; and if the Queen finds out, we’ll all be executed, you know. So, we are trying our best to fix it before she arrives.’ Just then, Five, glancing anxiously across the garden, cried out, ‘The Queen! The Queen!’ and the three gardeners immediately threw themselves flat on their faces. A sound of many footsteps echoed, and Alice turned eagerly to see the Queen.

First came ten soldiers carrying clubs; these were all shaped like the three gardeners, being oblong and flat, with hands and feet at the corners. Next were the ten courtiers, adorned all over with diamonds, walking two by two, like the soldiers. Following them were the royal children—ten in total—who merrily jumped along hand in hand, in pairs, each adorned with hearts. Next came the guests, mostly Kings and Queens, including the White Rabbit, who, talking nervously and smiling at everything, passed by without noticing Alice. Lastly, the Knave of Hearts appeared, carrying the King’s crown on a crimson velvet cushion, and finally came *The King and Queen of Hearts*.

Alice was uncertain whether she should lie down like the three gardeners but couldn’t recall any rule saying one must do so at a procession. ‘What’s the use of a procession,’ she mused, ‘if everyone has to lie down and can’t see it?’ So, she stood still and waited.

When the procession reached Alice, they all halted and looked at her. The Queen asked severely, ‘Who is this?’ She directed the question at the Knave of Hearts, who merely bowed and smiled in response.

‘Fool!’ the Queen said, tossing her head impatiently. She turned to Alice and continued, ‘What’s your name, child?’

‘My name is Alice, if it pleases your Majesty,’ Alice responded politely, but then thought to herself, ‘Why, they’re just a pack of cards! I needn’t be afraid of them!’

‘And who are *these*?’ the Queen pointed at the three gardeners lying by the rose-tree. As they lay face down, the pattern on their backs made it impossible for her to discern whether they were gardeners, soldiers, courtiers, or three of her own children.

‘How should I know?’ Alice retorted, surprised by her newfound boldness. ‘It’s none of *my* business.’

The Queen turned crimson with rage and, glaring at Alice like a wild animal, screamed, ‘Off with her head! Off —’

![“Off with her head!”](000013.png)

‘Nonsense!’ Alice responded loudly and firmly, silencing the Queen.

The King laid his hand on her arm and timidly interjected, ‘Consider, my dear: she’s only a child!’

The Queen angrily turned away from him and commanded the Knave, ‘Turn them over!’

The Knave carefully did so, with one foot.

‘Get up!’ shouted the Queen in a shrill voice. The three gardeners sprang up and began bowing to the King, the Queen, the royal children, and everyone else.

‘Stop that!’ screamed the Queen. ‘You’re making me dizzy.’ Then, turning to the rose-tree, she demanded, ‘What *have* you been doing here?’

‘May it please your Majesty,’ Two said humbly, kneeling, ‘we were just trying —’

‘Oh, I see!’ said the Queen, examining the roses. ‘Off with their heads!’ The procession continued, with three soldiers left behind to carry out the unfortunate gardeners, who ran to Alice for protection.

‘You shan’t be beheaded!’ Alice said as she placed them into a large flower-pot nearby. The three soldiers wandered around for a moment, searching for them, and then quietly marched off after the others.

‘Are their heads off?’ shouted the Queen.

‘Their heads are gone, if it pleases your Majesty!’ the soldiers answered.

‘That’s right!’ cried the Queen. ‘Can you play croquet?’

The soldiers fell silent, looking at Alice, as the question was clearly directed at her.

‘Yes!’ Alice shouted.

‘Then come on!’ roared the Queen, and Alice joined the procession, wondering what would happen next.

‘It’s a very fine day!’ said a timid voice beside her. It was the White Rabbit, anxiously peering into her face.

‘Very,’ Alice replied. ‘— Where’s the Duchess?’

‘Hush! Hush!’ said the Rabbit in a low, hurried tone, glancing nervously over his shoulder. He stood on tiptoe, leaned in close to her ear, and whispered, ‘She’s under sentence of execution.’

‘What for?’ asked Alice.

‘Did you say “What a pity?”’ the Rabbit inquired.

‘No, I didn’t,’ Alice clarified. ‘I don’t think it’s a pity at all. I asked “What for?”’

‘She boxed the Queen’s ears—’ the Rabbit began, and Alice let out a little laugh. ‘Oh, hush!’ the Rabbit whispered, looking frightened. ‘The Queen will hear you! You see, she came a bit late, and the Queen said —’

‘Get to your places!’ thundered the Queen, prompting people to scatter in all directions, bumping into one another. However, they settled down within a minute or two, and the game began.

![Alice trying to play croquet with flamingo and hedgehog](000014.png)

Alice had never seen such a peculiar croquet-ground; it was filled with ridges and furrows. The balls were live hedgehogs, the mallets were live flamingoes, and the soldiers had to double over and balance on their hands and feet to create the arches.

Alice initially found it challenging to manage her flamingo; she managed to get its body tucked comfortably under her arm with its legs hanging down. But just as she had the neck nicely straightened to strike the hedgehog, it would twist and look up at her with a puzzled expression, causing her to laugh uncontrollably. Whenever she got its head down and tried to start again, it was annoying to discover that the hedgehog had rolled away. To add to the chaos, there was usually a ridge or furrow blocking her path, and as the doubled-up soldiers meandered off to other parts of the ground, Alice concluded that it was indeed a very difficult game.

The players all engaged at once without taking turns, arguing and fighting over the hedgehogs. Before long, the Queen was in a furious rage, stomping about and shouting ‘Off with his head!’ or ‘Off with her head!’ every minute.

Alice began feeling quite uneasy; although she hadn’t yet clashed with the Queen, she knew it could happen at any moment. ‘And then,’ she worried, ‘what would happen to me? They’re terribly fond of beheading people here; the real wonder is that anyone is still alive!’

In her search for an escape route, she noticed a curious appearance in the air; it puzzled her at first, but after watching for a moment, she realized it was a grin. ‘It’s the Cheshire Cat! Now I’ll have someone to talk to!’

‘How are you getting along?’ said the Cat once there was enough of it for conversation.

Alice waited until the eyes appeared, then nodded. ‘It’s pointless to speak to it,’ she thought, ‘until its ears show, or at least one of them.’ Within a minute, the entire head was visible, and Alice put down her flamingo, eager to recount the game; she was glad to have someone to listen. The Cat seemed to think that there was enough of it in sight, as no more appeared.

‘I find they don’t play fairly,’ Alice lamented in a somewhat whining tone, ‘and they all argue so thoroughly one can’t hear oneself speak—there seem to be no particular rules; at least, if there are, nobody follows them—and it’s confounding to have everything alive; just look at the arch I need to go through next—it’s wandering about at the other end of the ground—and I should have croqueted the Queen’s hedgehog just now, but it ran away when it saw mine coming!’

‘How do you find the Queen?’ asked the Cat in a low voice.

‘Not at all,’ Alice replied. ‘She’s terribly—’ Just then, she noticed the Queen right behind her, listening. So, she quickly added, ‘— likely to win, so it hardly seems worthwhile to finish the game.’

The Queen smiled and moved on.

‘Who *are* you talking to?’ the King approached Alice, eyeing the Cat’s head with great curiosity.

‘It’s a friend of mine—a Cheshire Cat,’ Alice introduced. ‘Let me introduce you.’

‘I don’t like how it looks at all,’ said the King. ‘However, it may kiss my hand if it wishes.’

‘I’d rather not,’ remarked the Cat.

‘Don’t be rude,’ said the King, ‘and don’t look at me like that!’ He moved behind Alice as he spoke.

‘A cat may look at a king,’ Alice declared. ‘I read that somewhere, though I can’t recall where.’

‘Well, it must be removed,’ said the King decisively, calling for the Queen as she passed by, ‘My dear! I wish you’d have this cat removed!’

The Queen had only one way to resolve all dilemmas, large or small. ‘Off with his head!’ she announced, without even glancing back.

‘I’ll fetch the executioner myself,’ declared the King eagerly, and rushed off.

Alice thought she might as well return to see how the game was progressing, especially since the Queen’s voice echoed in the distance, raging with fury. She had already witnessed the Queen sentence three players to death for neglecting their turns, and she felt uneasy about how everything was unfolding, as the game was chaotic enough that she could never tell if it was her turn. So, she set out to find her hedgehog.

The hedgehog was engaged in a squabble with another one, which seemed to Alice a splendid chance to croquet one with the other. The only issue was that her flamingo had wandered across to the other side of the garden, where she saw it attempting helplessly to fly up into a tree.

By the time she managed to catch the flamingo and return, the quarrel between the hedgehogs had ended, and both were now out of sight. ‘But it doesn’t matter much,’ thought Alice, ‘since all the arches are gone from this side of the ground.’ So, she tucked the flamingo under her arm to keep it from escaping again and went back for a bit more conversation with her friend.

When she returned to the Cheshire Cat, she was surprised to find a sizable crowd gathered around it: there was a dispute among the executioner, the King, and the Queen, all speaking at once, while everyone else remained quiet and looked very uncomfortable.

As soon as Alice appeared, all three appealed to her to settle the argument, repeating their points simultaneously, making it very difficult for her to understand exactly what they were saying.

The executioner argued that you couldn’t behead someone without a body to behead. He insisted he had never done such a thing before and wasn’t going to start at *his* age.

The King claimed that anything with a head could be beheaded and that nonsense shouldn’t be tolerated.

The Queen insisted that if something wasn’t done about it in next to no time, she’d have everyone executed, which was why the entire group looked so grim and anxious.

Alice could think of nothing else to say except, ‘It belongs to the Duchess: you’d better ask *her* about it.’

‘She’s in prison,’ the Queen told the executioner. ‘Fetch her here!’ And the executioner dashed off like an arrow.

The Cat’s head began to fade the instant he left, and by the time he returned with the Duchess, it had completely disappeared. Meanwhile, the King and the executioner ran about in a frenzy, searching for it, while the rest of the group returned to the game.
